1 · Purpose & design principles

The Information & Public Relations (I&PR) department runs periodic citizen-perception surveys on government campaigns across districts. Each survey gives a satisfaction score (0–100) for a campaign, in a district, for a period. This methodology attributes those citizen scores to the officer responsible for that work at that time.

PrincipleHow we achieve it
Right scores to the right officerThe post's Office Status decides which campaigns/districts apply (rules below).
Credit only for time servedA survey counts only for the overlap between its period and the officer's tenure in the post.
Longer postings count morePosts are combined by a tenure-days weighted average.
Already on a 0–100 scaleCitizen scores are 0–100, so no rescaling is needed — averages stay 0–100.

2 · The raw data

Two tables drive the score:

  • Perception scores — one row per campaign × district × period with a citizen score (0–100). The period is a date range (e.g. "Apr-25").
  • Campaign → Department/HoD map — tells us which department and HoD own each campaign, so a post can be linked to the campaigns it is responsible for.

3 · How posts map to surveys — the attribution rules

An officer holds a sequence of posts. Each post's Office Status (and, for districts, its department) decides exactly which perception rows apply:

RuleOffice StatusPerception rows attributed
2SecretariatAll campaigns of that Department, across every district.
3HoDAll campaigns owned by that HoD, across every district.
6HoD — Chief SecretaryWhole-of-government: every campaign in every district.
4District — Revenue deptThe Collector view: all campaigns happening in that district, any department.
5District — other deptOnly that department's campaigns, and only in that district.
Rules 2, 3 and 6 are state-wide (all districts) because Secretariat/HoD roles steer a programme everywhere. Rules 4 and 5 are district-confined because a field officer owns outcomes only in their own district. A Revenue district post (the Collector) owns the whole district; a line-department district post owns only its own department's work there.

4 · Within a post — overlap-days weighting

A post may map to many survey rows spanning different periods. An officer should be credited for a survey only to the extent their tenure overlapped that survey's period. So within a post we take an overlap-days weighted average:

Post Score = Σ ( scorer × overlapDaysr ) ÷ Σ ( overlapDaysr )

where overlapDaysr is the number of days the survey period of row r overlaps the officer's from-date → to-date in that post. A survey that ran mostly outside the officer's tenure contributes little; one fully inside it contributes fully. Rows with zero overlap are ignored.

5 · Across posts — the tenure-proportionate officer score

Each post yields its own score. To combine an officer's posts into one number we use a tenure-days weighted average, so a post held for years counts far more than one held for weeks:

Officer Score = Σ ( PostScorep × tenureDaysp ) ÷ Σ ( tenureDaysp )

The sum runs only over posts that actually have a perception score (posts in campaigns); posts with no linked survey are skipped and neither help nor hurt. tenureDaysp is the officer's days in post p (from-date to to-date, or to today if still serving).

Two levels of proportionality. First, within a post, surveys are weighted by overlap days; then, across posts, post-scores are weighted by tenure days. Both ensure the score reflects where the officer actually spent their time.

6 · A worked example

An officer with two relevant posts

Post 1 — Secretariat, Health Dept, held 600 days. Applies to all Health campaigns statewide. Overlap-days weighted average of those surveys = 78.0.

Post 2 — District Collector (Revenue), Guntur, held 200 days. Applies to all campaigns in Guntur. Overlap-days weighted average = 66.0.

Officer Score = (78.0 × 600 + 66.0 × 200) ÷ (600 + 200)
  = (46,800 + 13,200) ÷ 800 = 75.0 / 100

The longer Secretariat tenure rightly dominates, but the district stint still moves the final number.

Why overlap matters (within a post)

In Post 2, two Guntur surveys apply: one ran for 120 days inside the officer's tenure (score 60), another for 40 days (score 84).

Post 2 Score = (60 × 120 + 84 × 40) ÷ (120 + 40) = (7,200 + 3,360) ÷ 160 = 66.0

The longer-overlapping survey dominates — the officer is credited in proportion to the time they were actually in charge.

7 · Glossary of statistical terms

Perception score
A citizen-satisfaction rating (0–100) from an I&PR survey for one campaign, in one district, in one period.
Campaign
A government programme/initiative that surveys are run against; each is owned by a department and HoD.
Office Status
The nature of a post — Secretariat, HoD, or District — which determines the attribution rule.
Attribution rule
The mapping (rules 2–6) from a post to the set of perception rows the officer is credited with.
Overlap days
The number of days a survey's period intersects the officer's tenure in a post — the weight used within a post.
Tenure days
The number of days an officer held a post — the weight used across posts.
Weighted arithmetic mean
An average in which some values count more than others; used twice — overlap-weighted within a post, tenure-weighted across posts.
Proportionate score
The principle that an officer's score reflects exactly how much of each survey/post fell within the time they served.
