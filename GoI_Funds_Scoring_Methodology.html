1 · Purpose & design principles

The Government of India releases funds for Centrally Sponsored Schemes (CSS) — roads, schools, sanitation, welfare, etc. An officer's job is to draw that money down, route it to the field, and spend it on real outcomes without letting it sit idle. This score measures exactly that.

Four principles guide the design:

PrincipleWhat it meansHow we achieve it
Efficiency, not raw amountSpending ₹100 of ₹100 is perfect; spending ₹100 of ₹1,000 is poor.Every indicator is a ratio (percentage), so it is scale-free.
Difficulty mattersSpending a tiny fund well is easier than spending a huge fund well.A log-scaled volume factor rewards larger funds (Step 5).
Time mattersAn officer who held a post for 2 years owns its results more than one who held it 2 weeks.Tenure-weighting by days in post (Step 4).
Bounded & comparableEvery officer must land on the same 0–100 ruler.Each ratio is capped to [0, 100]; the composite is a weighted mean.

Only officers present in the Officers master table are scored. If fund data exists for someone not yet in the master, it stays hidden until that officer is added.

4 · Step 1 — The four performance indicators

For a Secretariat/HoD post we compute four efficiency ratios from its aggregated funds (A, T, I, E, U). Each is a percentage and capped to the range 0–100.

A · Allocation Utilization Efficiency (AUE) — the headline test

AUE = ( Expenditure ÷ GoI Allocation ) × 100  =  ( E ÷ A ) × 100

Of everything the Centre sanctioned, how much became real spending? This single ratio captures both drawing the money and spending it, which is why it carries the highest weight.

B · Fund Utilization Efficiency (FUE)

FUE = ( Expenditure ÷ Funds with Agencies ) × 100  =  ( E ÷ I ) × 100

Of the money already in agencies' hands, how much was actually put to work rather than parked?

C · Fund Transfer Efficiency (FTE)

FTE = ( Funds Transferred ÷ GoI Allocation ) × 100  =  ( T ÷ A ) × 100

Of the money promised, how much did the officer succeed in drawing down? Leaving money at the Centre is a missed opportunity.

D · Untapped Fund Score (UFS)

First the Untapped Funds Ratio (lower is better):

UFR = ( Untapped Funds ÷ Funds Transferred ) × 100  =  ( U ÷ T ) × 100

Because a lower UFR is good, we flip it into a positive "higher-is-better" score:

UFS = 100 − UFR
Direction matters. AUE, FUE, FTE and UFS are all "higher is better" after this flip, so they can be safely blended in one weighted sum.

5 · Steps 2 & 3 — Weights & the efficiency composite

The four indicators are combined by importance. Expenditure-against-allocation is the most consequential, idle/untapped money the least:

IndicatorSymbolWeightRationale
Allocation Utilization EfficiencyAUE40%End-to-end outcome — sanction to spending.
Fund Utilization EfficiencyFUE30%Did money in hand get used?
Fund Transfer EfficiencyFTE20%Did the officer draw the money down?
Untapped Fund ScoreUFS10%Penalty for leaving sanctioned money behind.
Total100%
Efficiency (ES) = 0.40·AUE + 0.30·FUE + 0.20·FTE + 0.10·UFS

Because every term is already on a 0–100 scale and the weights sum to 1, the composite ES is automatically a number between 0 and 100 — no extra rescaling needed.

6 · Step 4 — Tenure-proportional weighting

An officer typically held several relevant posts. We do not treat them equally — a post held for two years should count far more than one held for two weeks. So we compute each post's efficiency ESp separately and take a days-weighted average:

Officer ES = Σ ( ESp × daysp ) ÷ Σ ( daysp )

where daysp is the number of days the officer held post p (from its from-date to its to-date, or to today if still serving). This is a classic weighted arithmetic mean, the same technique used for the Public Perception and Swarnandhra KPI scores.

Posts that carry no GoI funds (e.g. an unrelated posting) are skipped entirely — they neither help nor hurt the score.

7 · Step 5 — Fund-size (difficulty) normalization

⚖️ The core fairness rule you asked for: spending a small fund fully is easy; spending a very large fund fully is hard. Two officers who both achieve 100% efficiency are not equal if one managed ₹5 Crore and the other ₹5,000 Crore.

The efficiency ratios above are deliberately scale-free, so on their own they would treat those two officers identically. To correct this we multiply each officer's efficiency by a difficulty (volume) factor derived from the total funds they were responsible for (V = total Allocation / Funds Available).

Why a logarithm?

Fund sizes are extremely skewed — a few officers handle thousands of crores, most handle far less. A raw proportion would let one giant department dwarf everyone. Taking the natural logarithm ln(V) compresses that range so differences in order of magnitude (₹10 Cr vs ₹100 Cr vs ₹1,000 Cr) are rewarded smoothly, without a single mega-fund hijacking the scale.

Min–max normalization to a 0–1 weight

Across the officers currently on screen, we map the smallest log-fund to 0 and the largest to 1:

VW = ( ln(V) − ln(Vmin) ) ÷ ( ln(Vmax) − ln(Vmin) )  ∈ [0, 1]

We then convert this to a multiplier that never drops below a floor, so a small-fund officer is discounted but not erased:

Difficulty factor = 0.70 + ( 1 − 0.70 ) × VW  ∈ [0.70, 1.00]
  • The officer handling the largest fund gets the full ×1.00.
  • The officer handling the smallest fund gets ×0.70 — a 30% discount, reflecting the lower difficulty.
  • Everyone else scales smoothly in between.
The 0.70 floor is a deliberate choice: it ensures a genuinely excellent small-fund manager still scores respectably, while a large-fund manager who performs equally well is ranked above them.

8 · Step 6 — The final score

GoI Funds Score = Officer ES × Difficulty factor  (capped to 0–100)

Officers are then ranked by this score. Because the difficulty factor uses the on-screen cohort, the comparison stays fair whenever you filter by cadre, source, domicile or post-type — everyone is always judged against the group currently visible.

9 · District posts — handling only two fields

District data has only two numbers: Funds Available (FA) and Expenditure (E). We therefore cannot compute FTE (no allocation-vs-transfer split), nor FUE, nor untapped funds. The honest, defensible approach is a single indicator that mirrors the headline AUE:

DUE (District Utilization Efficiency) = ( Expenditure ÷ Funds Available ) × 100

This is treated as that post's efficiency ESp. It is then:

  • Tenure-weighted exactly like head-office posts (Step 4), and
  • Difficulty-scaled using Funds Available as the volume V (Step 5), so a district managing ₹2,000 Cr is not equated with one managing ₹20 Cr.
Mixed careers blend cleanly. If an officer held both head-office and district posts, each post contributes its own efficiency (4-indicator for Sec/HoD, single DUE for District), and the tenure-weighted average naturally combines them into one number before the difficulty factor is applied.
Why not invent the missing district fields? Fabricating "transferred" or "untapped" values we don't have would manufacture false precision. Scoring districts on what is genuinely measured — did the money in hand get spent? — keeps the result honest and comparable.

10 · Worked numerical examples

Example 1 — A Secretariat officer (single post, 2 years)

Aggregated scheme funds: A = ₹800 Cr, T = ₹720 Cr, I = ₹300 Cr, E = ₹650 Cr, U = ₹80 Cr.

Suppose this officer's ₹800 Cr is mid-large in the cohort, giving VW = 0.80, so Difficulty factor = 0.70 + 0.30×0.80 = 0.94.
Final score = 89.39 × 0.94 = 84.03 / 100.

Example 2 — Why fund size changes everything

Two officers each achieve a perfect efficiency ES = 100:

OfficerFund handled (V)VW (cohort)Difficulty factorFinal score
Officer X₹12 Cr (smallest)0.000.7070.0
Officer Y₹4,500 Cr (largest)1.001.00100.0

Both spent every rupee available, yet Officer Y — who did it with a fund 375× larger — finishes well ahead. This is precisely the bias-correction requested: spending small money well is not the same as spending big money well.

Example 3 — A District Collector (single post)

District aggregate: Funds Available = ₹260 Cr, Expenditure = ₹208 Cr.

DUE = 208 ÷ 260 × 100 = 80.0  →  ES = 80.0

If ₹260 Cr gives VW = 0.45 in the cohort, Difficulty factor = 0.70 + 0.30×0.45 = 0.835.
Final score = 80.0 × 0.835 = 66.8 / 100.

Example 4 — Mixed career (tenure blending)

An officer held a Secretariat post for 540 days (ES = 89.4) and later a District post for 180 days (ES = 80.0).

Officer ES = (89.4 × 540 + 80.0 × 180) ÷ (540 + 180) = (48,276 + 14,400) ÷ 720 = 87.05

The longer, stronger Secretariat tenure rightly dominates. The difficulty factor (from total funds handled) is then applied once to this blended efficiency.

11 · Glossary of statistical & scientific terms

Efficiency ratio
An output divided by an input (e.g. Expenditure ÷ Allocation), expressed as a percentage. It is scale-free: it measures how well, independent of how much.
Normalization
Re-expressing different quantities on one common scale so they can be compared or combined fairly. We use two kinds: capping each ratio to 0–100, and min–max normalizing fund size to a 0–1 weight.
Min–max normalization
Rescaling values so the smallest becomes 0 and the largest becomes 1, using (x − min) ÷ (max − min). Used here on the log of fund size.
Logarithm (natural log, ln)
A function that compresses very large numbers. Because fund sizes span several orders of magnitude, ln keeps one giant fund from dominating, while still rewarding bigger responsibilities.
Weighted arithmetic mean
An average where some items count more than others. We weight each post's efficiency by its tenure in days, so long postings influence the score more than brief ones.
Difficulty (volume) factor
A multiplier between 0.70 and 1.00 that scales an officer's efficiency by how large a fund they managed — the mechanism that stops small-fund and large-fund performance being treated as equal.
Capping (clamping)
Forcing a value to stay within a fixed range. Every indicator is clamped to [0, 100] so an unusual data point (e.g. expenditure temporarily exceeding the agency balance) cannot distort the score.
Cohort-relative scoring
The difficulty factor is computed against the officers currently on screen. Filtering the list re-bases the comparison so everyone is always judged against their visible peers.
Direction of an indicator
"Higher-is-better" (benefit) vs "lower-is-better" (cost). The Untapped Funds Ratio is a cost, so we convert it to the Untapped Fund Score (100 − UFR) before blending, keeping all four indicators in the same direction.
